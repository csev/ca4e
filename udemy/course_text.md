# 1. LEARNING OBJECTIVES

* Explain how transistor manufacturing evolved from discrete components to integrated circuits
* Describe photolithography and its role in modern chip fabrication
* Understand how CMOS transistors form basic logic gates
* Analyze how logic gates combine to perform binary arithmetic operations
* Describe how digital circuits store data using latches and feedback loops
* Explain how gates scale into components like adders, registers, and CPUs

---

# 2. TARGET AUDIENCE

This course is designed for learners who are curious about how computers work at a deeper level, beyond software. It is ideal for beginners to intermediate students in computing, engineering, or related fields who want to understand the physical and logical foundations of modern hardware.

It is also well suited for programmers, educators, and self-learners who want to connect abstract concepts like binary numbers and logic with the real hardware systems that execute programs.

# 3. COURSE LANDING PAGE

## Course Title

Computer Hardware History: From Analog to Digital

## Course Subtitle

Learn hardware fundamentals: transistors, VLSI, logic gates, and digital systems

## Course Description

This course explores how modern computer hardware evolved from early analog components to today’s highly integrated digital systems. You will follow the progression from individual transistors to complex microprocessors, gaining insight into both the physical construction and logical design of computing systems.

We begin with the historical development of transistor manufacturing, including the shift from discrete components to planar processes and integrated circuits. You will learn how photolithography enabled the creation of increasingly dense and powerful chips, leading to very large-scale integration (VLSI).

From there, the course transitions into how hardware is designed at a logical level. You will explore CMOS transistors and how they form the foundation of logic gates such as AND, OR, NOT, and NAND. Using these gates, we build up to digital circuits that perform useful tasks like binary addition.

Next, you will learn how computers store data using feedback-based circuits such as latches and registers. These concepts introduce the idea of state and memory, which are essential for building larger systems.

Finally, the course connects these building blocks into higher-level components like adders and registers, showing how they combine to form the core of a CPU. By the end, you will have a clear understanding of how simple physical elements scale into the complex hardware systems that run modern software.

# 5. LECTURE CONTENT

## Lecture: Evolution of Transistor Manufacturing

Description:
Explores the shift from discrete transistor assembly to planar manufacturing. Covers photolithography, integrated circuits, and how scaling enabled modern microprocessors and high transistor counts. 

---

## Lecture: Introduction to VLSI Design Concepts

Description:
Introduces very large-scale integration and how circuits are created using layout tools. Explains layers, vias, and how CMOS transistors are formed through material intersections. 

---

## Lecture: VLSI Layout Tool Walkthrough

Description:
Demonstrates how to construct circuits using a simplified layout tool. Covers drawing layers, connecting components with vias, and building a basic NOT gate from transistors. 

---

## Lecture: Building a NAND Gate in VLSI

Description:
Walks through constructing a NAND gate using CMOS design. Explains PMOS and NMOS arrangements, routing inputs, and validating behavior using truth tables. 

---

## Lecture: From Gates to Computer Architecture

Description:
Introduces how logic gates form the foundation of CPUs. Covers memory, input/output, and how digital signals move through hardware systems to execute programs. 

---

## Lecture: Binary Numbers and Base-2 Addition

Description:
Explains binary number representation and how addition works in base two. Introduces carry behavior and prepares for building arithmetic circuits with logic gates. 

---

## Lecture: Half Adders and Full Adders

Description:
Covers how logic gates implement addition. Builds half adders and full adders, showing how carry signals propagate and enable multi-bit arithmetic. 

---

## Lecture: Building a Half Adder Circuit

Description:
Demonstrates constructing a half adder using XOR and AND gates. Shows how inputs produce sum and carry outputs and verifies results with truth tables. 

---

## Lecture: Digital Gate Simulation Tools

Description:
Explores a gate simulation environment. Covers components, wiring, labeling, and building circuits such as adders and displays for binary values. 

---

## Lecture: Building an AND Gate Circuit

Description:
Shows how to construct and test a simple AND gate using inputs and outputs. Includes labeling, truth table validation, and automated grading concepts. 

---

## Lecture: Storing Data with Latches

Description:
Introduces SR latches and feedback loops for storing binary values. Explains how circuits maintain state over time and the role of clock-controlled D latches.
